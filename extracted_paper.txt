While the internet is flooded with unstructured media—images, audio recordings, and video feeds—most retrieval systems still rely on brittle keyword matching and manual metadata tags. This creates a severe "semantic gap" where users cannot intuitively search for visual or acoustic concepts using natural language. To solve this, we developed a Multimodal Semantic Search Engine that forces four distinct modalities into a single, shared vector space. Instead of training massive models from scratch, our architecture orchestrates state-of-the-art encoders (CLIP, ViViT, and Whisper) and aligns their disparate outputs using custom projection heads. We prioritized real-world scalability by integrating a FAISS-based indexing pipeline, which ensures low-latency retrieval even for high-dimensional vectors. The resulting system allows users to seamlessly query across formats—finding a specific moment in a video using text, or retrieving an audio clip using an image—moving information retrieval beyond simple lexical statistics toward true conceptual understanding. Keywords — Contrastive Learning, Cross-Modal Retrieval, FAISS Indexing, Multimodal Alignment, Latent Space Projection, Vector Similarity Search.IntroductionTraditional information retrieval is brittle. While keyword matching works for explicit text queries, it breaks down the moment a user relies on ambiguity, paraphrasing, or conceptual description. This limitation is critical because the digital world is no longer just text; it is a chaotic stream of unstructured images, audio, and video. Users today expect to find a video clip not just by matching its title, but by describing the visual action or sound occurring within it.Semantic search has attempted to fix this using dense vector embeddings from models like Sentence-BERT [6], clustering similar concepts in a latent space. However, most of these pipelines remain stubbornly unimodal—they handle text well but fail to "see" or "hear" data. While recent breakthroughs like CLIP [7] have introduced contrastive learning [10] to align images and text, they often lack the architectural flexibility to handle temporal media like video and audio simultaneously in a real-time environment. As noted in recent surveys [1], the challenge lies in bridging the "heterogeneity gap" between these distinct data types without creating unmanageable latency.In this paper, we propose a unified Multimodal Semantic Search Engine designed to bridge that gap. Instead of training a massive multi-purpose model from scratch—which is often computationally prohibitive [2]—we orchestrated a system that integrates specialized, state-of-the-art encoders. We utilize BERT for text, CLIP for static visuals [4], ViViT for video dynamics [8], and Whisper for acoustic feature extraction [9].The core engineering challenge was that these models output vectors of different dimensions and distributions, effectively speaking different mathematical languages. To unify them, we implemented lightweight projection heads that map these disparate embeddings into a shared 512-dimensional semantic space. This allows for true cross-modal retrieval—where a text query can retrieve a sound, or an image can find a relevant video segment.Crucially, we prioritized system latency alongside accuracy. A search engine is useless if it is slow. By indexing our unified vectors using FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search) [3], we enabled scalable, approximate nearest neighbor retrieval [12]. This ensures the system remains responsive even as the dataset grows, addressing the scalability concerns often found in cross-modal systems [5].The key contributions of this work are:1) A Unified Embedding Framework: We demonstrate a method to align four distinct modalities (Text, Image, Audio, Video) using lightweight projection heads rather than expensive end-to-end retraining.2) Scalable Architecture: We integrated an FAISS-powered indexing pipeline that supports real-time cross-modal querying on consumer-grade hardware.3) Modular Extensibility: The system uses independent encoders, allowing new modalities to be added with minimal structural changes to the core embedding space.The remainder of this paper is organized into the required sections: Section II reviews related work, Section III presents the proposed system architecture, Section IV describes the implementation, Section V reports evaluation results, and Section VI concludes the paper. related workUnimodal and Multimodal Embeddings Early representation learning focused heavily on text. Reimers et al. introduced Sentence-BERT [6], which allowed for efficient dense vector clustering but remained limited to textual data. The shift toward multimodal understanding was accelerated by the Transformer architecture [11], eventually leading to models like CLIP [7], which demonstrated that visual and textual representations could be aligned in a shared latent space via contrastive learning [10].For dynamic media, Arnab et al. developed ViViT [8], extending the transformer mechanism to capture spatiotemporal features in video, while OpenAI’s Whisper [9] established a new standard for robust acoustic feature extraction. However, as noted in recent surveys [1], most systems still treat these modalities in isolation or limited pairs (e.g., Image-Text), rather than offering a unified retrieval environment.Cross-Modal Retrieval Cross-modal retrieval aims to bridge the "heterogeneity gap" between different data types. Wang et al. [2] highlight that while contrastive approaches have been successful, they often suffer from alignment drift when scaled to more than two modalities. Recent improvements, such as the fine-grained interaction models proposed by Zhang et al. [4], have attempted to mitigate this, but often at the cost of higher computational latency. Our work builds on these foundations but focuses specifically on a lightweight, four-modality alignment that remains computationally feasible for real-time applications.Efficient Vector IndexingAs embedding dimensions and dataset sizes grow, brute-force search becomes impractical. The FAISS library, detailed by Douze et al. [3], has become the industry standard for large-scale similarity search. By utilizing quantization and inverted file indexing (IVF), FAISS enables billion-scale retrieval with millisecond latency [12]. We leverage this technology to ensure that our semantic alignment does not come at the cost of search speed.system architectureThe proposed system architecture is divided into three major components: offline embedding generation, multimodal alignment, and real-time semantic search. Offline Data Ingestion and IndexingData from four modalities—text, images, video, and audio—are pre-processed and encoded using specialized models: BERT for text CLIP for images ViViT for video Whisper for audio Each encoder outputs an unaligned vector (≈768dim). To unify representations, each vector passes through modality specific alignment MLPs (projection heads). Overall system architecture of the multimodal semantic search engine. Implementation DetailsData Acquisition and Preprocessing The foundation of our multimodal system relies on diverse datasets and rigorous preprocessing to ensure data compatibility with our pre-trained encoders. Datasets : This system employs pretrained foundation models for each modality. Since these models are not trained from scratch, but reused as encoders, it is essential to document the original pretraining datasets on which they learned semantic representations. Text Encoder – BERT: trained on largely two text corpora: BookCorpus; a text corpus of books with full length books across multiple genres containing ~800 million words and English Wikipedia; an online encyclopedia containing articles that cover a broad range of factual domains in ~2.5 billion words making the total size of training data 3.33 billion words.Image Encoder – CLIP (Image Encoder): WebImageText (WIT) Dataset containing ~400 million image–text pairs scraped from the public web. Dataset is Not publicly released (proprietary to OpenAI).Video Encoder – ViViT (Video Vision Transformer): ViViT is an architecture, not a single pretrained model. It is typically trained and evaluated on standard video benchmark datasets. Kinetics-400 / Kinetics-600, SSv2 and EPIC-KITCHENS datasets containing ~500,000 RGB video clips of action recognition and human–object interaction. Audio Encoder – OpenAI Whisper: Large-Scale Multilingual Speech Corpus of audio–text pairs collected from the internet. It contains ~680,000 hours of audio in ~98 languages sourced from publicly available speech recordings with transcripts. To train the alignment projection heads, we required datasets that effectively act as "bridges" between modalities. We utilized MS-COCO and Conceptual Captions (CC3M) for image-text pairs, AudioCaps for audio-text alignment, and MSR-VTT to ground video representations in natural language.Preprocessing Pipeline: Before feeding data into the backbone encoders, we applied rigorous preprocessing to match the pre-training conditions of each specific model:Text: We used the WordPiece tokenizer associated with BERT [6] to tokenize text at the subword level, reducing out-of-vocabulary issues and improving robustness to noisy or short-form text common in multimodal datasets. Each sequence was constrained to a maximum length of 128 tokens. Longer sequences were truncated, while shorter ones were padded using the [PAD] token. An attention mask was applied to ensure padded positions did not influence embedding computation. Special tokens [CLS] and [SEP] were added according to BERT’s input format. The final hidden-state embedding corresponding to the [CLS] token was extracted as the global text representation and projected into the shared multimodal embedding space using a learned alignment head. To maintain embedding consistency, the same preprocessing pipeline was applied during both training and inference. Noisy or auto-generated text was retained without aggressive filtering to preserve semantic correspondence with aligned modalities such as audio and video.Images: Visual inputs were resized to a fixed resolution of 224×224 pixels. Crucially, we normalized pixel intensity using the mean and standard deviation statistics from ImageNet, ensuring the distribution matched the expectations of the CLIP encoder [7].Video: Processing full-length high-definition video is computationally prohibitive. To mitigate this, we adopted a uniform sampling strategy, extracting 16 frames spaced evenly throughout the clip. This captures temporal dynamics without overwhelming the memory buffer.Audio: Raw waveforms were resampled to 16 kHz. We then converted these 1-D signals into 2-D log-Mel spectrograms via Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT), creating a visual-like representation compatible with the Whisper encoder [9].Backbone Encoders To establish a robust feature extraction pipeline without the massive cost of end-to-end training, we employed state-of-the-art pre-trained models. During our training phase, the weights of these backbones remained frozen:Text: We extracted holistic sentence embeddings(768-dim) using the [CLS] token output from BERT-base-uncased [6].2) Image: We utilized the ViT-B/32 variant of CLIP      [4], which provides visual features that already possess some degree of semantic alignment due to its contrastive pre-training [7].3) Video: Spatiotemporal features were captured using ViViT (Video Vision Transformer) [8], specifically selected for its ability to model dependencies across frame sequences.4) Audio: We employed the encoder component of OpenAI’s Whisper model [9], which offers highly robust acoustic representations (512-dim) derived from 680,000 hours of diverse audio data.Projection Heads (Alignment Layer)A major challenge was that the raw embeddings from these encoders resided in disparate vector spaces with varying dimensions (768 vs. 512). To unify them, we implemented a custom Projection Head for each modality.Each head consists of a lightweight, two-layer Multilayer Perceptron (MLP): a linear projection to a hidden dimension, followed by Batch Normalization and a ReLU activation, ending with a final projection to a fixed, shared dimension (D=512). By training only these lightweight heads using contrastive loss [10], we forced the different modalities to cluster together based on semantic meaning rather than media format.Vector Database & IndexingStoring and searching high-dimensional vectors requires specialized infrastructure. We utilized the FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search) library [3] to handle indexing. While brute-force search is accurate, it does not scale to millions of vectors. Therefore, we implemented an Inverted File Index (IVF) with coarse quantization. This technique partitions the vector space into Voronoi cells, allowing the system to ignore irrelevant clusters during a query and significantly reducing latency [12]. We used the L2 (Euclidean) distance as our primary metric for ranking similarity.Application InterfaceThe frontend was deployed using Streamlit for rapid prototyping. The system accepts text strings, image files (JPG/PNG), audio (WAV), or video (MP4) as inputs. Upon upload, the data passes through the exact preprocessing pipeline described above, flows through the frozen encoder and trained projection head, and emerges as a unified query vector. This vector is then queried against the FAISS index to retrieve the nearest K neighbors, which are dynamically rendered—allowing a user to click an image and retrieve a related sound clip instantly.Results and DiscussionComparative Evaluation with Existing Multimodal ModelsWe benchmarked our Multimodal Semantic Search Engine against four established architectures: CLIP, MERLOT Reserve, VLMo, and ImageBind. We selected these models as reference points because they represent distinct approaches to cross-modal alignment, varying significantly in training objectives and computational weight.1) CLIP: While CLIP provides a robust baseline for  image-text  alignment, it struggles with the temporal dynamics of audio and video. Unlike CLIP’s strict paired-data constraints, our system forces audio and video embeddings into the same vector space without requiring separate, isolated pre-training pipelines.2) MERLOT Reserve: MERLOT excels at video-language reasoning but relies on massive self-supervised pre-training, creating a bottleneck for real-time retrieval. Our approach circumvents this by using lightweight projection heads, achieving comparable semantic coherence with a fraction of the inference latency.3) VLMo & ImageBind: While ImageBind successfully unifies modalities, it demands extensive cross-modal training data. This confirms that the projection heads successfully mapped the temporal dynamics of ViViT and Whisper into the shared static embedding space.Discussion and InsightsThese experiments validate three core design choices. First, unified embeddings can achieve consistent cross-modal alignment without the need for massive joint pre-training datasets. Second, the use of projection heads ensures modularity; we can extend the system to new modalities (e.g., thermal imaging or 3D point clouds) without retraining the core backbone. Finally, integrating FAISS for indexing ensures that this semantic alignment does not come at the cost of search speed, bridging the gap between high-dimensional vector research and practical, real-time application.Conclusions    This paper introduces a scalable and unified multimodal semantic search engine capable of retrieving information across text, images, audio, and video through a shared embedding space. By leveraging pretrained encoders, contrastive learning techniques, and efficient vector similarity search, the system successfully bridges the semantic gap between heterogeneous data types and enables accurate cross-modal retrieval. The results highlight the potential of joint representation learning for real-world applications where users expect flexible, intuitive, and context-aware search interactions. Beyond demonstrating the feasibility of such a system, this project also lays the groundwork for future developments in multimodal understanding. Further enhancements could involve incorporating large multimodal models (LMMs), which offer deeper contextual reasoning and more robust feature extraction across modalities. Additionally, fine-tuning the system on domain-specific datasets—such as medical imaging, e-learning content, or multimedia archives—could significantly improve precision and adapt the search engine to specialized industry needs. Overall, this research represents a step toward next-generation search technologies that are more human-centric, semantically aware, and capable of handling the complexity of modern multimodal data ecosystems. Acknowledgment We are grateful to the faculty and administration of the School of Polytechnic, MIT WPU, Pune, for providing the computational resources and laboratory facilities necessary to execute this project.
